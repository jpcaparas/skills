# Principles

Taste is not decoration. It is the ability to make the right thing feel inevitable for the product, the task, and the user's level of focus.

## Core Rules

1. **Fit the job before the mood.**
   - A launch page can afford spectacle.
   - A workflow surface must prioritize clarity, sequence, and recovery.

2. **Make one thing dominant on every screen.**
   - One primary action, one primary claim, or one primary object.
   - If five things compete, none of them feel important.

3. **Build structure before expression.**
   - Fix layout, spacing, order, and grouping first.
   - Then adjust palette, materiality, and motion.

4. **Use contrast to clarify, not to show effort.**
   - Contrast can come from size, weight, spacing, edge definition, or pace.
   - Color is only one tool.

5. **Let surfaces mean something.**
   - Use borders, fills, blur, and shadows only when they help users parse layers.
   - Avoid decorative chrome that does not explain hierarchy.

6. **Design the full state model.**
   - Idle, hover, focus, pressed, selected, loading, empty, success, error, disabled.
   - Most interfaces feel cheap because only the happy path is designed.

7. **Treat copy as visual material.**
   - Good typography cannot rescue vague labels.
   - Short, specific language makes layouts feel more confident.

8. **Reduce the vocabulary.**
   - Reuse radii, shadow behaviors, line treatments, spacing intervals, and icon weights.
   - Identity comes from disciplined repetition.

9. **Motion should explain change.**
   - Use it to show entry, priority, cause and effect, or continuity.
   - If motion has no informational job, lower it or remove it.

10. **Leave some things plain on purpose.**
    - Not every region needs the same amount of art direction.
    - Quiet zones make the designed moments feel intentional.

## Planning Questions

Answer these before proposing a visual system:

1. What is the product category?
2. What is the user's goal on this screen?
3. How often will they see it?
4. Are they browsing, deciding, operating, or monitoring?
5. Should the screen calm them down, energize them, or get out of the way?
6. What would still work if imagery disappeared?

## Taste Axes in Practice

Use the axes from `../SKILL.md` to make the direction concrete:

- High composure + high density:
  - strong fit for admin tools, desktop software, and data-heavy work
- Low density + medium motion:
  - strong fit for launches, premium consumer onboarding, and editorial product pages
- Sharp contrast + low materiality:
  - strong fit for precise product UIs and operational tools
- Soft contrast + layered materiality:
  - strong fit for warm consumer apps or brand-forward products

## What Good Direction Looks Like

A strong direction can be summarized in four sentences:

1. The product should feel like `X`, not `Y`.
2. The user should notice `A` first, then `B`.
3. The screen should stay mostly `calm/sharp/soft/dense`, with expression concentrated in `specific places`.
4. The design must hold up with `real constraints`.

If you cannot state the direction that cleanly, the direction is not ready.
